As Vietnam continues its economic integration on the global stage, the country’s accounting landscape is undergoing a significant transformation. The impending adoption of International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) alongside the existing Vietnam Accounting Standards (VAS) marks a pivotal shift in financial reporting practices for businesses operating in the country.

This guide provides timely insights into the key differences between IFRS and VAS, the benefits of IFRS adoption and the challenges businesses may face during this transition.

## Overview of the standards

As cross-border commercial activities are becoming the norm, internationalisation and diversification of production, trade and global services are changing the way we undertake business. Investors and governments see an increased need to apply international best practices across the global economy.

More than a third of all financial transactions occur across borders, and this number is expected to grow in the next decade. Therefore, investors with international operations in multiple countries, raising capital and undertaking transactions are seeing rising difficulties in maintaining their internal financial systems efficient and compliant across multiple jurisdictions.

When private investors and public stakeholders use local financial statements to represent each jurisdiction in making economic decisions, the need for an international standard to be accepted across multiple jurisdictions has become key for driving growth and innovation. In the context of accounting as a global business language, financial accounting standards enable state management agencies and investors to have a tool to evaluate and compare financial information between entities using the same set of rules and common standards for making commercial decisions in a timely manner.

International Financial Reporting Standards are recognised as the most common accounting language today, widely applied for accounting work in 168 countries and territories worldwide. IFRS was designed and developed by an independent, non-profit organisation, the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) and officially came into use in 2001 to replace the International Accounting Standards (IAS), a set of international standards applied since 1973.

Meeting the diverse requirements of businesses, as well as investors, countries often allow businesses to choose National Financial Reporting Standards or IFRS when preparing and presenting financial statements. In Vietnam, the Ministry of Finance has issued Vietnam Accounting Standard (VAS), which is built based on international accounting standards, suitable to the conditions and level of economic development of Vietnam and put into use from 2001 to present.

According to the World Bank’s recommendation, countries should prioritise adopting IFRS rather than developing accounting practices according to their own conditions because these standards are widely accepted and applied flexibly in integrating world best practices.

Over the years, the World Bank has accepted IFRS as the basis for presenting customers’ financial statements. The application of IFRS will create conditions for enterprises to be eligible to list on the international market or receive preferential loans from international financial institutions such as the World Bank, the International Monetary Fund and the Asian Development Bank.

To meet the needs of international integration and cross-border investment diversification, updating Vietnam’s accounting policies to be closer to the international standard has become an urgent priority.

On 16 March 2020, the Ministry of Finance issued Decision 345/QDBTC approving the Project “Application of Financial Reporting Standards in Vietnam” aiming to apply IFRS and the new Vietnamese Financial Reporting Standards (VFRS) from 2025.

Therefore, understanding the differences between these two sets of accounting standards IFRS and VAS is essential for businesses undertaking operations in Vietnam to prepare for the regulatory transformation in the next few years.

### Benefits of the IFRS

IFRS bring transparency by enhancing the international comparability and quality of financial information, enabling investors and other market participants to make informed economic decisions.

IFRS strengthens accountability by reducing the information gap between capital providers and those to whom they have entrusted their money. These Standards provide the information needed to hold management accountable. As a source of globally comparable information, IFRS are also vital to regulators around the world.

IFRS contributes to economic efficiency by helping investors identify opportunities and risks across the world, thus improving capital allocation. For businesses, using a single, trusted accounting language lowers the cost of capital and reduces international reporting costs.

## 2026 IFRS roadmap in Vietnam

### Detailed structures

Vietnam Accounting Standards (VAS) include:

- 26 Vietnam Accounting Standards (VAS) issued by Vietnam Ministry of Finance from 2001 to 2005
- Circular 99/2025/TT-BTC for corporate accounting
- Circular 202/2014/TT-BTC for consolidation
- Other applicable regulations by the MoF

Current VAS Standards are primarily based on the old versions of IAS with certain customisations to fit Vietnam’s circumstances. VAS standards have not been revised/ amended since their publications.

**VAS is a rule‑based, cost‑oriented accounting regime designed primarily for statutory compliance.**

International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) include:

- IAS: 24 standards (released from 1973 to 2001)
- IFRS: 17 standards (released after 2001)
- Interpretations from SIC and IFRIC
- IFRS are frequently updated and revised.

IFRS Standards follow a principles-based approach.

## IFRS vs VAS

Below is a brief explanation of the two standards.

### Preparation of financial statements

#### 1\. Presentation of financial statements

| **IAS 1** | **VAS 21** |
| --- | --- |
| A complete set of financial statements includes:<br>- Statement of financial position (balance sheet)<br>- Statement of profit or loss and other comprehensive income<br>- Statement of changes in equity<br>- Statement of cash flows<br>- Notes to financial statements<br>- Comparative information<br>IAS 1 allows expenses to be presented on the face of a statement of profit or loss either _by nature_ or _by function_. | A complete set of financial statements includes:<br>- Balance sheet<br>- Income statement<br>- Cash flows statement<br>- Notes to financial statements<br>Changes in equity are disclosed in the notes on owners’ capital under VAS.<br>Companies reporting under VAS are required to apply the standardised chart of accounts and financial statements format.<br>VAS requires expenses to be presented _by function_ on the face of the statement of profit or loss and to be disclosed by nature in the notes. |

#### 2\. Statement of cash flows

| **IAS 7** | **VAS 24** |
| --- | --- |
| Bank overdrafts are included in cash and cash equivalents.<br>Disclosure of changes in financing-related liabilities must occur apart from changes in other assets and liabilities.<br>Cash flows from hedging activities are classified as investment activities except when they are for trading purposes (which are classified as operating activities) or when a contract is accounted for as a hedge of an identifiable position. In this case, the cash flows of the contract are classified in the same manner as the cash flows of the position being hedged. | Under VAS 24 and Circular 99, bank overdrafts are generally presented as short‑term financial liabilities, similar to bank loans, unless they are integral to cash<br> management |

#### 3\. Events after the reporting period

| **IAS 10** | **VAS 23** |
| --- | --- |
| Under IAS 10, an entity is obligated to adjust for profit sharing or bonus payments that are determined after the reporting period if there is a legal or constructive obligation to make such payments due to events occurring before that date. | Profit‑sharing or bonus payments are adjusted only if there is a legal obligation established by the reporting date. Amounts approved or determined after the reporting period are generally treated as non‑adjusting events. |

#### 4\. The effects of changes in foreign exchange rates

| **IAS 21** | **VAS 10** |
| --- | --- |

#### 5\. Related party disclosures

| **IAS 24** | **VAS 26** |
| --- | --- |
| IAS 24 defines various classes of entities and people as related parties and sets out the disclosures required with respect to those parties.<br>The concepts of _control_ and _significant influence are_ used to assess the relationship.<br>IAS 24 states that below are not related parties:<br>- Two entities simply because they have a director or other member of key management personnel in common or because a member of key management personnel of one entity has significant influence over the other.<br>- Two joint venturers simply because they share joint control of a joint venture.<br>- Providers of finance, trade unions, public utilities and departments and agencies of a government that do not control, jointly control or significantly influence the reporting entity simply by their normal dealings with an entity (even though they may affect the freedom of action of an entity or participate in its decision-making process).<br>- A customer, supplier, franchisor, distributor or general agent with whom an entity transacts a significant volume of business simply by the resulting economic dependence.<br>(IAS 24.11) | VAS 26 is based on the previous version of IAS 24 (1994). Decree 132/2020/ND-CP supplements the definitions of related parties by using a threshold to assess the relationship.<br>- A company is engaged in capital transfers, receipts of capital transfer of at least 25% of the contributed capital in the tax period with an individual who manages or controls the company or who has family relationships.<br>- A company is engaged in borrowing or lending at least 10% of the contributed capital at the time of transaction with an individual who manages or controls the company or has family relationships.<br>- The two businesses are operated or controlled by human resources, finances and business operations by individuals who are close members of a family<br>(Decree 132, Article 5.)<br>Decree 132/2020/ND-CP states that below are still considered as related parties:<br>- Two enterprises have more than 50% of the management board members in common or have the same member of the management board with the right to decide on financial policies or business activities appointed by a third party.<br>- Two enterprises are considered as related parties if they enter into the transaction of borrowing or lending at least 25% of the owner’s contributed capital, equivalent to more than 50% of the company’s total mid- and long-term loans at the time of the transaction.<br>(Decree 132, Article 5.) |

### Revenue recognition

#### 1\. Revenue from contracts with customers

| **IFRS 15** | **VAS 14 & VAS 15** |
| --- | --- |
| IFRS 15 became effective on 1 January 2018, replacing both IAS 11 (Construction contracts) and IAS 18 (Revenue).<br>IFRS 15 provides one comprehensive revenue recognition model for all customer contracts to improve comparability within industries, across industries and in capital markets.<br>Revenue is recognised when control of a good or service transfers to a customer.<br>IFRS 15 requires a five-step process to be applied before revenue can be recognised:<br>- Identify the contract with a customer.<br>- Identify the separate performance obligations in the contract. Performance obligations are promises in a contract to transfer to a customer distinct goods or services.<br>- Determine the transaction price. The transaction price is the amount of consideration an entity expects to be entitled to in exchange for transferring promised goods or services to a customer.<br>- Allocate the transaction price to each performance obligation based on the relative stand-alone selling prices of each distinct good or service promised in the contract.<br>- Recognise revenue when a performance obligation is satisfied by transferring a promised good or service to a customer.<br>(IFRS 15:7) | VAS 14 and VAS 15 are based on the previous versions of IAS 18 and IAS 11, respectively.<br>A number of guidance under IFRS 15 have been included in VAS and Circular 99, such as allocating revenue to free goods sold in bundle, deferring revenue to match with the subsequent free goods under loyalty programs, etc. Revenue is recognised when risks and rewards are transferred to the buyer<br>VAS 14 provides five conditions for recognising revenue from the sale of goods and four conditions for recognising revenue from the provision of services.<br>VAS 15 provides guidance to recognise revenue from construction contracts.<br>Additionally, Circular 99 provides detailed accounting guidance of revenue recognition of real estates and traditional customer programs, considering variable considerations in construction contracts. (Circular 200, Article 79) |

### Income taxes

| **IAS 12** | **VAS 17** |
| --- | --- |
| IAS 12 requires an entity to recognise a deferred tax liability or (subject to specified conditions) a deferred tax asset for all temporary differences, with some exceptions as follows:<br>- Initial Recognition of goodwill, assets and liabilities<br>- Investments in subsidiaries, associates and joint ventures if control over timing exists.<br>- Taxable temporary differences related to undistributed profits unless control over timing exists and a reversal is probable.<br>A deferred tax asset is recognised for deductible temporary differences, subject to the following conditions:<br>- If it is probable that taxable profit will be available against which the deductible temporary differences can be utilised.<br>- Except when the deferred tax arises from the initial recognition of an asset or liability in a transaction that is not a business combination and does not affect accounting or taxable profits.<br>- Additionally, for deductible temporary differences arising from investments in subsidiaries, branches, associates and interests in joint arrangements, it is recognised only to the extent that the temporary difference will probably reverse in the foreseeable future and taxable profit will be available. | VAS 17 is based on the previous version of IAS 12.<br>Under VAS 17, deferred tax liability is recognised for all taxable temporary differences, except when arising from the initial recognition of an asset or liability in a transaction that does not affect profit. VAS 17 remains silent on temporary differences related to business combinations and goodwill.<br>Circular 202 supplements and requires that a deferred tax liability is recognised for all taxable temporary differences unless the deferred tax liability arises from the initial recognition of an asset or a liability of a transaction that does not affect accounting profit or taxable profit (or tax loss) at the time of the transaction.<br>VAS 17 also requires the recognition of deferred tax assets for all deductible temporary differences with exceptions similar to IAS 12 but is silent in VAS 17 on temporary differences arising from a business combination. |

### Fixed assets

#### 1\. Tangible fixed assets

| **IAS 16** | **VAS 03** |
| --- | --- |
| An item of Property, Plant and Equipment (PPE) that qualifies for recognition as an asset shall be measured at its cost, without any minimum capitalisation threshold.<br>IAS 16 allows two accounting models: Cost model and Revaluation model. (IAS 16.29 & 31)<br>IAS 16 requires an entity to determine the depreciation charge separately for each part of a PPE item with a cost that is significant in relation to its total cost.<br>Under IAS 16, the residual value and useful life is required to be reviewed at least at each financial year-end and if there is any change, it will be accounted for as a change in accounting estimate. | The company can set a capitalisation threshold for a fixed asset at VND 30 million or more.<br>Only cost model is allowed under VAS 3. #### 3\. Investment property

| **IAS 40** | **VAS 05** |
| --- | --- |
| An investment property shall be measured initially at its cost. #### 4\. Impairment of assets

| **IAS 36** | **No equivalent VAS** |
| --- | --- |
| IAS 36 – Impairment of Assets aims to ensure that an entity’s assets are carried at no more than their recoverable amount.<br>All assets within the IAS 36 scope are tested for impairment where there is an impairment indicator. #### 5\. Lease accounting

| **IFRS 16 (replaced IAS 17 effective from 1 January 2019)** | **VAS 6** |
| --- | --- |
| IFRS 16 (replaced IAS 17 effective from 1 January 2019) defines a lease as a contract, or part of a contract, that conveys the right to use an asset (the underlying asset) for a period of time in exchange for consideration.<br>Under IFRS 16, lessees have to recognise a lease liability IFRS 16 requires lessees to recognize a lease liability representing future lease payments and a ‘right-of-use asset’ for nearly all lease contracts.<br>Lessees will report interest expenditure associated with the lease liabilities and right-of-use asset depreciation in the income statement. | Leases are required to be classified as either:<br>- Finance leases (that transfer substantially all the risks and rewards incidental to ownership and give rise to asset and liability recognition by the lessee and a lease receivable by the lessor)<br>- Operating leases (that result in expense recognition by the lessee, with the asset still being recorded in the lessor’s books).<br>Finance lease assets are recorded as assets on the balance sheet and are depreciated accordingly, and depreciation expense is recorded in the statement of business activities.<br>VAS 06 and Circular 99 do not adopt the IFRS 16 right‑of‑use model. |

### Financial instruments and fair value

#### 1\. Fair value

| **IFRS 13** | **No equivalent VAS** |
| --- | --- |
| IFRS introduces the concept of _fair value_, which is unfamiliar to companies complying with VAS.<br>IFRS 13 defines fair value as _The price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date_.<br>A fair value measurement requires management to determine four things:<br>- The particular asset or liability that is the subject of the measurement<br>- The highest and best use for a non-financial asset<br>- The principal (or most advantageous) market<br>- The valuation technique<br>IFRS 13 establishes a fair value hierarchy categorised into three levels to provide inputs to valuation techniques used to measure fair value. The hierarchy from highest priority is as follows:<br>- Level 1: Quoted prices in active markets<br>- Level 2: Inputs that are observable for the asset or liability, either directly or indirectly.<br>- Level 3: Inputs that are unobservable inputs for the asset or liability. | Accounting Law 88, issued in 2015, introduces the concept of fair value, with the instruction that the MoF will issue further specific guidance.<br>No comprehensive guidance on fair value has been issued to date.<br>Circular 99 provides limited and transaction‑specific guidance on fair value measurement, without establishing a fair value hierarchy, valuation techniques, or consistent measurement principles. In detail:<br>- Listed shares: fair value determined based on quoted prices on organised securities markets.<br>- Shares traded on UPCOM: measured based on observable transaction prices or prices on the transaction date where available.<br>- Unquoted shares: measured at transaction price or cost and subsequently carried at cost less impairment, rather than re‑measured at fair value. |

#### 2\. Financial instruments

| **IFRS 9** | **No equivalent VAS** |
| --- | --- |
| IFRS 9 (replaced IAS 39) addresses the classification, measurement and derecognition of financial assets and financial liabilities.<br>IAS 32 – Financial instruments: Presentation<br>IFRS 7 – Financial instruments: Disclosures IFRS 9 introduces a new model for recognising impairment losses – the expected credit losses (ECL) model.<br>IFRS 9 has three classification categories for debt instruments:<br>- Amortised cost<br>- Fair value through other comprehensive income (FVTOCI)<br>- Fair value through profit or loss (FVTPL) | There is no VAS standard equivalent to IFRS 9, IAS 32 and IFRS 7.<br>However, Circular 99 provides limited, rule‑based guidance on the presentation and accounting of certain financial instruments, such as convertible bonds and treasury shares, but does not establish an integrated financial‑instrument framework comparable to IFRS.<br>VAS has not adopted ECL model, instead, provisioning under guidance of Circular 48/2019/TT‑BTC is still applied.<br>Investments in shares and debt instruments are classified by nature and purpose (e.g. trading investments, long‑term investments, investments in subsidiaries, associates, and joint ventures) rather than under an IFRS‑style business‑model assessment.<br>These investments are generally carried at historical cost and are only written down when there is evidence of decline in value; fair‑value remeasurement is not applied. |

Under IFRS 9, the expected credit losses (ECL) model is a _three-stage approach_ based on the change in the credit quality of financial assets since initial recognition.

### Business combinations

#### 1\. Separate financial statements

| **IAS 27** | **Guidance on corporate accounting system – circular 200** |
| --- | --- |
| IAS 27 does not mandate which entities should produce separate financial statements. An entity might be required by local legislation, or it might elect, to present separate financial statements. #### 2\. Consolidated financial statements

| **IFRS 10** | **VAS 25** |
| --- | --- |
| A parent entity is not required to present consolidated financial statements if it satisfies any of the following conditions:<br>- The entity is a wholly-owned or partially-owned subsidiary whose minority shareholders, including those without voting rights, are informed about and do not object to the non-consolidation of financial statements.<br>- The entity’s securities, including debt and equity, are not traded publicly.<br>- The entity has not submitted, nor is it in the process of submitting, its financial statements to a securities commission or similar regulatory body for issuing any class of instruments in a public market.<br>- The entity’s ultimate or intermediate parent publishes consolidated financial statements that are available for public use and comply with IFRS, wherein the subsidiaries are either consolidated or valued at fair value through profit or loss in accordance with IFRS 10.<br>Additionally, an entity that meets the definition of an investment entity under IFRS 10 can account for its subsidiaries at fair value through profit or loss as per IFRS 9 rather than presenting consolidated financial statements. | Consolidated financial statements are governed by VAS 25 and Circular 202/2014/TT‑BTC<br>Consolidated financial statements are generally required when control exists. Exemptions from consolidation are limited and rule‑based, and are not aligned with IFRS 10 exemption criteria.<br>An entity may be exempt from preparing consolidated financial statements mainly when its parent entity prepares consolidated financial statements in accordance with VAS that include the reporting entity, subject to strict regulatory conditions.<br>VAS 25 and Circular 202 do not recognise the concept of an investment entity, and therefore do not permit measurement of subsidiaries at fair value through profit or loss in lieu of consolidation. |

#### 3\. Non-current assets held for sale and discontinued operations

| **IFRS 5** | **No equivalent VAS** |
| --- | --- |
| An entity shall classify a non-current asset (or disposal group) as held for sale if its carrying amount will be recovered principally through a sale transaction rather than through continuing use.<br>In general, assets (or disposal groups) held for sale are not depreciated, are measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell and are presented separately in the statement of financial position. | No comprehensive guidance on non-current assets held for sale and discontinued operations has been issued to date. |

#### 4\. Business combinations

| **IFRS 3** | **VAS 11** |
| --- | --- |
| Under IFRS 3, goodwill is not amortised and is subject to annual impairment review or, more frequently, if there is an indication of impairment.<br>Non-controlling interest (NCI) is measured either at the NCI’s proportionate share of the recognised amounts of the acquiree’s identifiable net assets or at fair value.<br>Under IFRS 3, contingent consideration is recognised at its fair value at the date of acquisition. | Defines borrowing costs as interest and other expenses an entity incurs in relation to the borrowing of funds. These costs can include:<br>- **Interest expense**: Calculated using the effective interest method outlined in IFRS 9.<br>- **Finance charges**: Related to finance leases recognized in line with IAS 17 Leases.<br>- **Exchange differences**: From foreign currency borrowings to the extent that they are considered an adjustment to interest costs. | VAS 16 is modelled after an earlier version of IAS 23.<br>- It does not mandate the use of the effective interest method for calculating interest expenses.<br>- It does not address exchange differences resulting from foreign currency borrowings as part of borrowing costs. |

## Conclusion

When it comes to enhanced credibility and comparability of financial information, IFRS are the set of high quality and globally accepted accounting standards that could do this job effectively and globally. As Vietnam is becoming preferred destination for FDI, the need to implement IFRS is more imminent than ever before.

IFRS-VAS convergence in Vietnam will not only be mandatory from 2025 but it will also enhance financial reporting capabilities of businesses operating in Vietnam having head office in other countries. For other businesses, this will provide more transparency and comparability and will enhance their chances of M&A activities with international partners. This convergence will benefit both domestic and foreign stakeholders, especially investors and regulators. This would also facilitate the integration of Vietnamese capital market with the global market and at the same time, reducing the operating costs and complexities faced by the accounting departments by eliminating the need to convert financial statements on periodic basis.

Apart from benefits from such convergence, businesses need to evaluate the challenges that they may encounter during the process, such as readiness level, lack of resources (qualified accountants) and availability of information to enable the convergence. Most of these could be addressed through training, while others need professional support.

In order to reap the true benefits and address the challenges at each stage, it is important for businesses to hire professionals who have expertise in both IFRS and VAS. Alternatively, services from a professional firm could make this transition rewarding
